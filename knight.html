Trade the Knight

Puzzle Overview

The "Trade the Knight" challenge is a 5x5 graph traversal problem designed to test algorithmic pathfinding and optimization. Competitors must navigate a grid of mathematical operations using only legal chess Knight moves to maximize their final score.

The Rules

  • The Starting State: You begin on the center square (the Knight) with a starting value of 1.
  • The Movement: You may only move in an "L-shape," identical to a legal Knight's move in chess (two squares in one direction, then one square perpendicular).
  • No Repeats: You must visit every single square on the 5x5 board exactly once. You cannot land on the same square twice.
  • The Objective: Apply the mathematical operation in each cell to your running total as you land on it. Find the specific 25-square path that results in the maximum possible final value.

Hints

  • Hint 1: Greed isn't always good. Taking paths that maximize your score early might lead you to a dead end. Sometimes a deliberate dive into the negatives is exactly what you need to set up a massive multiplier later on.
  • Hint 2: Work backwards from the largest multipliers on the board. Since the goal is the absolute maximum, you likely want your running total to be acted upon by the most impactful operations right near the end of your tour.
  • Hint 3: A knight's tour must visit every square exactly once. You can't just hover around the positive numbers; you are forced to visit the negative multipliers. Turn them to your advantage by ensuring your score is negative when you land on them.

The Winning Path (Square Indices):
[13, 2, 11, 22, 19, 10, 3, 6, 17, 24, 15, 4, 7, 16, 23, 20, 9, 18, 25, 14, 5, 8, 1, 12, 21]

The Math (Step-by-Step Traversal):
The genius of this specific path lies in the final 8 moves. Because 1766 is the product of a prime number (883 x 2), the algorithm builds a "trap." It intentionally drives the score into the deep negatives just to flip the sign with a massive multiplier at the very end, before precision-landing on 1766.
